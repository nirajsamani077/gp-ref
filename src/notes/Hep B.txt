Hep B 

dsDNA virus 
Spread by body fluids, vertical transmission 
Up to 20 week incubation 
Can cause some acute Hep A like initially 

Chronically 
- chronic hepatitis (up to 10%) —> liver failure (1%)
- HCC
- GN
- Vasculitis (PAN)
- Cryoglobulinaemia 

Hep B a very important vaccine 
- given routinely x 3 in childhood (2,3,4 months)
- At risk workers, activities & CLD patients 
- Recombinant DNA vaccine, 3 doses per course 
- Anti-HBs marks response after 3 months 
—> <10 = non-responder, test for current or past infection, full course again & if still not HBIG
—> 10-100 = partial, one further dose 
—> >100 = full responder, booster after 5 years 

Treating Hep B
- PEG IFN-a which reduces viral replication (better response in younger women with high liver inflammation, low viral loads & HIV negative)
- Newer agents include tenofivir etc
